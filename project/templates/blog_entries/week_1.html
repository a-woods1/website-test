Welcome to New York!
WEEK 1

If you're going to run a marathon, make sure you take the last few weeks off.

It may sound counterintuitive, but the concept of “tapering” is a critical part of marathon training. Tapering is the idea of reducing your training in the last few weeks in order to make you stronger---both mentally and physically---for the big day.

Our team has been doing our own version of “tapering” this week by using our time together to refuel, reflect, and re-energize for our marathon summer ahead.

Refuel: How can we quickly acclimate to a new culture?

This summer, we’re thrilled to be working onsite with our clients in their New York City office. This has involved new apartments, new work culture, and new work technology (including the intimidatingly efficient Terminal). Although we were already familiar with our client's work culture from our spring semester research, inhabiting the space on an ongoing basis has already felt different, and will ideally lead us to even more insights about how our client works.

Jinping in our new office space, which has a slightly better view than our room in Pittsburgh!


Reflect: How do we optimize our team dynamic?

To continue our marathon metaphor, the end of spring semester felt like falling across the finish line. By the last day, we were all drained. Our team spent a considerable amount of time this week discussing how to avoid this in the summer by creating strong, inclusive habits around work distribution, group input, and decision making.

We made some cards to keep in our room that any team member can “play” at any time to ensure we are living these habits.

Ask three questions

When offering criticism, we will commit to asking at least three questions. This will not only help us better understand the thinking behind the criticism, but also allow us to be more generative.

The QIE triangle

When making key decisions, surface the tension between Q (quality), I (inclusion) and E(efficiency). Using this card ensures we think about the current balance of all three elements.

Use the whiteboard

When decision making discussions have become a verbal back and forth, use this card to get everyone to draw their ideas at the whiteboard so we can all visualize what we are thinking.

Decision time

When a discussion or meeting has gone on for too long without a decision being made, the person who uses this card must propose a way to reach a decision.

Individual - Group - Individual

This card will facilitate better working sessions by going through an individual - group - individual process. This process gives individuals time to digest information before reaching a team agreement.

Make it together

When two ideas/hypotheticals are in conflict, use this card to get both parties to create something with elements of both (in Sketch, on the whiteboard, in slides, etc.)

We will continue to treat team issues like design problems, and invest more time up front to set us up for greater success later on.

Re-energize: how will we move forward with our current design prompt?

Our team had a great discussion (at the whiteboard!) to confirm our focus for the semester.

Our goal: To support people with disabilities to disclose what they need to get hired and perform.

Questions involved in the disclosure experience currently include:

  • Why should I disclose my disability?
  • Who should I disclose to?
  • When is disclosure necessary?
  • How should I disclose?
  • Should I disclose at all?

Among these questions, our team finds disrupting “whether to disclose” the most interesting, as it involves giving more power to the person with a disability. We want to help people with disabilities make a more informed decision during their own disclosure experience. Some current measures of success for us will be making people with disabilities feel supported, eliminating surprises, and removing uncertainty during the disclosure experience.

Another commitment for us this semester will be more making and research by design.
