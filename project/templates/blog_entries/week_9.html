If We Could Turn Back Time
WEEK 9

How often do you get the chance to do a project over again? Well, due to the nature of our Capstone, we find ourselves in a somewhat similar place to where we were at the end of Spring semester. However, this time around we have the benefit of hindsight! Here are three key things we are doing differently this time around when it comes to polishing off client deliverables:

Take advantage of the IGI model

Okay, so IGI may or may not be a technique that we made up, but this language and approach has been really useful to us in thinking through workflow for major deliverables. IGI stands for Individual, Group, Individual. The idea is to give group members individual work time to work on pieces of a deliverable, then use group time to synthesize these pieces into one common vision. Once this group common vision is in place then we can assign individual ownership to different parts of that vision for completion.


For example, at the end of Spring semester, we had one owner for the team presentation. That person put together a complete outline of the entire presentation themself, and drove much of the work on editing and refining the presentation based on others’ comments. Not only did this put a lot of pressure on one person, but it also made other group members feel they didn’t have a true voice in the presentation. This time around, using IGI we’ve created much better work distribution and sense of ownership.

During the Group section, we collaboratively drew out every slide in the presentation to have one map that we agree on as a team


Make it usable

At the end of Spring, we also ran into some issues with dependency and single points of failure. For example, a single Sketch file contained our service blueprint, and it was unclear which version was the most up to date. Additionally, we created a visual style that was really dependent on the skills of one person. Our team website had a code setup that made it difficult for straight CSS editing.


This time around, we’re doing three new things:

  1. Ensuring we have the right tools for sharing, updating and merging our work (besides Github we have been using Abstract and Zeppelin for design work)
  2. Building structure into work that allows for easy manipulation. For example, highlighting elements in React code that enable easier identification for CSS styling.
  3. Leveraging more templates for visual styling. Below are examples of the initial variations we made for our final presentation in Google slides, that anyone can copy and update with their own content

Three variations of a visual template for our final presentation



Hear from the horse’s mouth

Finally, it sounds simple, but we’re being more proactive about asking our clients and faculty for their opinions. Last semester we sort of disappeared into a hole for two weeks and came out the other end with a presentation. This time around, we’re scheduling more check ins and soliciting more feedback ahead of time to ensure that what we deliver meets (and hopefully exceeds!) expectations.
