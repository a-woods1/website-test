Divergence Before The Convergence
WEEK 7

Assuming you’ve made it this far in our blog series, you are probably not a stranger to the double diamond:

Diagram credit to Marcelo Fagundes and Murilo Gomes of Insitum


The double diamond is a process diagram that outlines the widening and narrowing of ideas, thoughts and work that occurs during the design process. As you may have guessed, we are more towards the end of this double diamond than the beginning. Our team is currently at the end of the Develop stage, meaning we are working on a variety of different iterations, with the goal of narrowing our focus to deliver something to our clients by the end of the next four weeks. Here are three key things that we’ve been developing:

Developing designs for candidates with disabilities

Can you spot the difference between the three designs below? This is one example of how we have created multiple variations within an in-room preview experience for candidates with disabilities, in order to test which one provides the most helpful information (spoiler alert, it’s a combination between one and two!)


By creating a variety of designs, we are now able to narrow and focus on the one that works best for users. We’ve also created multiple variations of the accommodations menu for candidates, including experimenting with using images versus text descriptions. One interesting finding for us has been that some people with disabilities find accommodation-related images patronizing. For example, showing an image of an accessible bathroom seems to imply to some people with disabilities that we don’t think that they know what an accessible bathroom looks like. Whether or not to include images will be an interesting design decision for us, since we know that some candidates with cognitive impairments might prefer visual information over text.

Developing client-centric user paths

While our primary focus is building out an experience for candidates with disabilities, we also want to think holistically about what is happening on our client’s side of the experience. If a candidate requests an accommodation, it means nothing if they don’t actually receive it. This week we diverged by thinking through key flows for three distinct user groups:

  • Recruiters and recruiting coordinators: These roles communicate directly with candidates, and oversee implementation of any accommodations.
  • Employee relations: This team helps determine what a reasonable accommodation would be in the workplace.
  • Hiring managers: These people decide what will be required as part of interviewing for positions on their teams.
After a meeting with our client, we’ve decided to scope down and converge to just building out a design for recruiters and recruiting coordinators (while having back up logic to address the two other user groups).

Developing key measurement criteria

Finally, we’ve been steadily building our measurement criteria for what makes a successful design in user testing:

Basic - Can the user accurately understand the information in the tool? We started by asking users comprehension questions to ensure they could understand interview information within our interface.

Good - Can the user accurately evaluate what accommodations might be needed based on this information? Once we knew our design was good enough for comprehension, we wanted to ensure that the information was helpful for making a decision about whether or not an accommodation would be needed.

Better - Can the user easily request an accommodation? If our designs were now able to provide the right information, could they help users take action and easily request an accommodation?

Best - Does the user feel confident requesting an accommodation? Finally, if our design could give users the right information and help them accomplish their goal, we wanted to ensure the design made users feel confident in their decision to share accommodation needs, as the ultimate goal of our project is to make people with disabilities more confident about their disclosure decision.

We slowly tested more and more of these criteria during testing these last two weeks, and now in our upcoming sessions we will be able to just focus on confidence and measuring how our designs communicate trust.

Onwards, to Deliver!
